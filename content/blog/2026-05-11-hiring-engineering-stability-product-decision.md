A founder sits across the table from their investors and presents two hiring options. Option one: bring in someone to own the product roadmap, the strategic direction, the vision for what the platform becomes. Option two: bring in someone to stabilize the engineering team, fix the CI/CD pipeline, clean up the infrastructure, stop the bleeding.

The investors say: fix engineering first. It is the responsible choice. You cannot build a roadmap on an unstable foundation. Get the house in order, then think about where to go next.

This advice feels right. It is also a product decision disguised as a risk-management decision.

When you defer product leadership and hire only for engineering stability, you are making a specific bet. You are betting that the current direction is correct enough to stabilize around. You are betting that the founder's vision, held informally in their head and communicated through ad hoc conversations, is sufficient to guide engineering priorities for the next six to twelve months. You are betting that the engineering lead can decide what to build and how to build it simultaneously, without those two responsibilities creating a conflict.

These bets sometimes pay off. In my experience, they usually do not.

Here is what happens. The engineering lead comes in and does exactly what they were hired to do. They audit the infrastructure. They set up CI/CD. They introduce code review standards. They start mentoring junior developers. They stabilize deployments. All of this is necessary and valuable work.

But stabilize toward what? Without a product owner defining the destination, engineering stabilizes around the current state. The codebase gets cleaner. The pipeline gets faster. The tests get written. And the product stays exactly where it was, because nobody in the room has the authority or the mandate to say: this feature matters more than that one, and here is why, based on what customers need and where the market is moving.

The founder tries to fill this gap. They always do. They show up in standups with feature ideas. They send late-night Slack messages about something a customer said. They drop into sprint planning with a new priority that was not on the board yesterday. The engineering lead absorbs these inputs and tries to sequence them alongside the stability work. The result is a roadmap that oscillates between "fix the foundation" and "build the founder's latest idea," with no framework for deciding which one wins on any given week.

This is not a failure of the engineering lead. It is a structural gap. The two-pillar model, where product and engineering operate as peer functions with distinct mandates, exists for a reason. Product owns what gets built and why. Engineering owns how it gets built and at what quality. When you collapse both into one role, you get someone who is accountable for everything and has the authority to prioritize nothing.

I have seen this play out in companies with five engineers and companies with fifty. The scale changes. The dynamic does not. Without product leadership, engineering teams default to technical improvement. They refactor. They optimize. They pay down tech debt. All good things. But they are optimizing the machine without anyone confirming that the machine is pointed in the right direction.

The counterargument is always resources. "We cannot afford both hires." This is often true in terms of headcount. It is less true in terms of capability. Product leadership does not require a full-time VP from day one. It requires someone with the mandate to define priorities based on customer and market signal, even if that person is also doing other work. It requires making the role explicit, with clear ownership, rather than leaving it distributed across the founder's inbox and the engineering lead's best guesses.

The hardest version of this problem is the internal candidate. A strong engineer who has been with the company for years. They know the codebase. They know the customers. They know where the bodies are buried. Promoting them to engineering lead is the obvious, low-risk move. And it is often the right one for the engineering pillar. But it does not solve the product pillar. It just makes the absence less visible, because the internal candidate's deep context creates the illusion that product decisions are being made when they are really just being inherited from the founder's last conversation.

Investors who advise "stabilize engineering first" are not wrong about the problem. They are wrong about the sequencing. Stability without direction is maintenance. It keeps the lights on. It does not build the thing that makes the company worth more in eighteen months.

The question is not: can we afford to hire for product and engineering at the same time? The question is: can we afford to stabilize without knowing what we are stabilizing toward?

Every month spent optimizing the current state is a month where the product roadmap is implicit, informal, and living in the founder's head. That is not a plan. That is a liability. And the longer it persists, the harder it becomes to introduce product discipline later, because the engineering team has already built habits around a world where priorities arrive as interrupts instead of strategy.

Hire for stability. Yes. But define the destination before you start cleaning the road.
