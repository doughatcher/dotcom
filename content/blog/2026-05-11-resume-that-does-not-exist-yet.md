The job posting asks for a Shopify Plus expert. Or a Salesforce Commerce Cloud architect. Or an Adobe Commerce technical lead. Pick one.

The engagement needs all three.

This is the staffing problem that nobody in mid-market commerce is solving, because the career paths that produce the person who can solve it do not exist yet.

## What the work actually requires

A typical mid-market discovery in 2026 is not a single-platform conversation. The merchant has an existing platform, a target platform, and a constellation of ten to fifteen systems that have to work together: ERP, OMS, WMS, PIM, DAM, CDP, ESP, payments, tax, search, reviews, loyalty, subscription, identity. The platform decision is maybe twenty percent of the outcome. The integration architecture across the constellation is the other eighty.

The person who leads that work needs to hold fluency across multiple commerce platforms, because the integration patterns are different on each one. How you connect an OMS to Shopify Plus is not how you connect an OMS to SFCC. The data models are different. The event architectures are different. The extension points are different. The deployment models are different. The failure modes are different.

But that is table stakes. The person also needs to sit in a room with a merchant's VP of Operations and explain why the integration sequence matters, what the tradeoffs are, and what the realistic timeline looks like. Without a project manager translating. Without slides. In plain language, with enough commercial awareness to understand what the merchant is actually optimizing for.

That combination, deep technical fluency across platforms plus the ability to have a business conversation without a translator, is what the work demands. And the industry does not produce it.

## How the current career path works

Commerce technical architects are grown, not hired. The typical path: a developer spends three to five years going deep on a single platform. They learn its data model, its extension framework, its deployment quirks, its performance characteristics. They ship projects. They earn scars. They become the person the team calls when something breaks at two in the morning.

Eventually they move into an architect role. They lead technical design. They review code. They make integration decisions. They are the senior technical voice on the engagement.

This path produces someone who is very good at one platform and has opinions about the others based on what they have read, not what they have shipped. That was fine when engagements were single-platform. It is not fine now.

The engagements are multi-platform. The discovery spans three commerce stacks and fifteen integration targets. The technical architect who only knows one platform cannot lead the conversation. They can contribute to part of it. They cannot hold the whole picture.

## The rotation problem

The obvious solution is to rotate architects across platforms. Put your SFCC architect on a Shopify Plus project. Put your Shopify architect on an Adobe Commerce engagement. Let them build working fluency through direct experience.

This is the right answer. It is also extremely uncomfortable for delivery organizations.

Rotating a senior architect onto an unfamiliar platform means they are slower for the first engagement. Their utilization dips. Their confidence dips. The client is paying senior rates for someone who is learning. The delivery manager sees a risk. The account manager sees a risk. Everyone involved would prefer to staff the person who already knows the platform.

So the rotation does not happen. The architect stays in their lane. The lane gets deeper and narrower. And the gap between what the work requires and what the career path produces gets wider.

## What the merchant experiences

The merchant does not see this staffing problem. They see its effects.

They see a technical architect who is confident about the platform and vague about the integrations. They see integration decisions deferred to "the integration team" instead of being made in the architecture phase. They see a design document that describes the platform in detail and treats the constellation as an appendix.

Three months later, the integration work is where the project is behind schedule. The platform build is on track. The connections between systems are not. The merchant wonders why nobody flagged this earlier.

Nobody flagged it because the person in the architect seat did not have the cross-platform integration fluency to see it coming. They saw their platform clearly. They saw the constellation through a fog.

## What this means for hiring

If you are hiring a technical architect for a commerce engagement in 2026, the resume you need does not exist in the standard talent pool. The person who has shipped production code on three commerce platforms and led integration architecture across a fifteen-system constellation is rare. They are rare because the career path that produces them is accidental, not intentional.

The people who have this profile got it by moving between agencies, or by working at a merchant that replatformed twice, or by spending time in an advisory role that exposed them to multiple stacks. They did not get it through a structured career development program, because no such program exists.

This creates a hiring problem that most organizations solve by lowering their requirements. They hire the single-platform expert and hope the integration complexity will sort itself out. It does not.

## The uncomfortable implication

The mid-market commerce industry needs a new career path for technical architects. One that values cross-platform fluency alongside depth. One that rotates people through discomfort intentionally. One that treats "I have shipped on three platforms" as a qualification, not a sign of job-hopping.

Building that path requires delivery organizations to accept short-term utilization loss for long-term capability gain. It requires staffing models that value range over specialization. It requires clients to understand that the architect who knows their platform and two others is more valuable than the architect who only knows theirs.

None of this is technically difficult. All of it is organizationally hard. And until someone builds the career path deliberately, the gap between what the work demands and what the talent market supplies will keep widening.

The resume the industry needs does not exist yet. Someone has to start writing it.
